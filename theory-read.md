# Political Prism Theory: Concept Brief

You are building a website for a political quiz based on a triangular/prism model of politics.

This is not a normal political compass. Do not think in terms of “left/right” or “authoritarian/libertarian.” This model says politics comes from three deeper social virtues. Every person, ideology, regime, and civilization is a mixture of these three virtues.

The three virtues are:

1. Freedom
2. Equality
3. Stability

Each virtue answers a different question about society.

Freedom asks:
“What can the individual do?”

Equality asks:
“What is the individual’s standing among others?”

Stability asks:
“What can the society preserve, coordinate, and become as a collective body?”

The prism is about which virtue someone believes should dominate when virtues conflict.

## Freedom

Freedom centers the individual.

It cares about personal agency, exit, ownership, speech, association, sexuality, trade, risk-taking, self-direction, and the right to refuse the collective.

Its social logic is voluntary exchange: trade, contract, consent, competition, property, markets, and rule of law.

Freedom sees society as a network of individuals. The individual is morally prior to the group.

Its extreme form is anarcho-capitalism or radical individualism.

Its fear is being absorbed into the collective.

Its accusation against Equality:
“You will sacrifice the individual to make everyone the same.”

Its accusation against Stability:
“You will sacrifice the individual to preserve order.”

Its failure mode:
Fragmentation, predation, oligarchy, commodification, weak solidarity, and the strong exploiting the weak if no balancing force exists.

## Equality

Equality centers the person as a member of a moral community.

It cares about fairness, dignity, social standing, anti-domination, class, exploitation, inherited advantage, caste, welfare, universal provision, and whether people meet each other as equals or as masters and dependents.

Its social logic is family/care: sharing, mutual obligation, protection, moral inclusion, redistribution, compassion, and sometimes utopian promise.

Equality does not ask only “am I free?” It asks “free compared to whom, under what conditions, and with what power?”

Its extreme form is communism or Marx-Leninism.

Its fear is domination by wealth, class, birth, market power, tribe, race, inheritance, or private hierarchy.

Its accusation against Freedom:
“You call it freedom, but the weak are abandoned to the strong.”

Its accusation against Stability:
“You preserve unjust hierarchy and call it order.”

Its failure mode:
Enforced sameness, dependency, moral coercion, denial of excellence, suppression of exit, and systems that promise care but become controlling.

## Stability

Stability centers society as a collective body.

It cares about order, continuity, hierarchy, duty, borders, tradition, institutional memory, state capacity, loyalty, shared identity, long-term survival, and civilizational coherence.

Its social logic is command: hierarchy, office, rank, obedience, law, bureaucracy, monarchy, military structure, and collective discipline.

Stability sees society not merely as individuals or equals, but as a body that must survive across time.

Its extreme form is absolutism, tsarism, monarchy, empire, or authoritarian nationalism.

Its fear is collapse, decadence, chaos, rootlessness, internal division, and a society losing the ability to act as one.

Its accusation against Freedom:
“You dissolve the common world into selfish individuals.”

Its accusation against Equality:
“You weaken excellence, hierarchy, discipline, and inherited structure in the name of compassion.”

Its failure mode:
Tyranny, stagnation, caste, corruption, repression, blind obedience, and sacrifice of living people to the system.

## The Core Conflict

The three virtues are all real. None is fake. None is purely good or evil.

But they cannot all be maximized at once.

Maximum Freedom undermines guaranteed Equality and often weakens Stability.

Maximum Equality limits Freedom and often requires centralized enforcement.

Maximum Stability limits Freedom and accepts unequal hierarchy.

Politics is what happens when people rank these virtues differently.

The quiz is not asking:
“Are you left-wing or right-wing?”

It is asking:
“When society must choose, what do you protect first?”

## The Prism

The prism has multiple layers. Each layer shows the same triangle through a different lens.

### Virtues Layer

Corners:
- Freedom
- Equality
- Stability

This is the philosophical layer. It explains what each side values and protects.

### Units Layer

Corners:
- Individual
- Community
- Collective Body

This is the social/anthropological layer.

Freedom corresponds to the individual and trade.

Equality corresponds to community, family, care, and shared moral standing.

Stability corresponds to the collective body, hierarchy, command, and institutional continuity.

### Ideologies Layer

This layer shows political systems as mixtures.

Examples:
- Anarcho-capitalism: extreme Freedom
- Marx-Leninism: extreme Equality enforced through centralized power
- Tsarism/Absolutism: extreme Stability/Authority
- Liberalism: Freedom with some Equality and institutional Stability
- Social democracy: Equality balanced with Freedom and Stability
- Conservatism: Stability with some Freedom
- Nationalism: Stability plus collective identity
- Libertarianism: Freedom with minimal Stability and little enforced Equality
- Fascism: Stability/collective body taken to domination
- Republicanism: Freedom plus civic Stability, anti-caste but not necessarily egalitarian in outcome
- Monarchy: Stability through inherited hierarchy

These placements should be presented as interpretive, not mathematical truth.

### Figures Layer

This layer places historical and modern thinkers/leaders as reference points.

The goal is orientation, not worship or denunciation.

Use figures from many traditions: theorists, rulers, revolutionaries, economists, religious leaders, reformers, and modern politicians.

Label placements as approximate.

## Tone

The site must steelman each virtue.

Do not make Freedom look like selfishness only.

Do not make Equality look like envy only.

Do not make Stability look like tyranny only.

Each pole should feel compelling to someone who believes in it.

The best one-sentence summaries are:

Freedom:
“Do not make me a tool of society.”

Equality:
“Do not let society divide people into masters and dependents.”

Stability:
“Do not dissolve the order that lets a people survive.”

## Design Meaning

The triangle is not decoration. It is the theory.

The user should feel that every label, color, explanation, and result comes from this same three-way conflict.

The site should feel like a serious civic-philosophical instrument: clear, readable, sharp, and elegant.

Avoid meme energy. Avoid partisan ranting. Avoid overexplaining. Make the model instantly graspable, then let users explore deeper.

## Quiz Meaning

The quiz should locate the user inside the prism by measuring which virtue they protect when tradeoffs appear.

A good question should force tension.

Not:
“Do you like freedom?”

Better:
“Should people be allowed to make risky choices even if many will ruin themselves?”

Not:
“Do you like equality?”

Better:
“Should society correct inherited advantage even when doing so limits property rights?”

Not:
“Do you like stability?”

Better:
“Should institutions preserve continuity even when rapid change would satisfy current public demand?”

The result should not just give a score. It should explain the user’s political instinct:

- What they protect first.
- What they tolerate second.
- What they are most willing to sacrifice.
- What their blind spot may be.
